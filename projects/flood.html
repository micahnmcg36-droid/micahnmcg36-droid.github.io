About This Project

For this project, I used data from FEMA.gov. I wanted to use various time-series methods to forecast future flood insurance damages.

Data Cleaning, Transformation, and Stationarity Testing

First, I removed everything except for the dates and claim amounts. I made sure there were no missing values. I also made sure to format the dates in the Pandas DateTime format. Next I looked at the data, and I noticed that it would probably be best to take the logarithm of the dependent variable (which requires removing dates with 0 claims). I then did a stationarity test (Augmented Dickey Fuller), and found that the series was stationary, and I could begin my analysis.

ARIMA and SARIMA

Plotting the Autocorrelations and Partial Autocorrelations, I decided to begin with an ARMA(2,1) model. The model had decent performance (MAPE of 8.06%), but I thought I could better forecast the data using a seasonal term, as one would expect flood damages to have a seasonality component. From there I used a Seasonal ARIMA model with the same values for p and q, and found slightly improved performance. When I looked at it though, I thought that I could do even slightly better by taking into account the spikes (for this, I used an indicator for the 95th percentile). This performed the best of the three in terms of MAPE.

SVR

Finally, I used SVR (Support Vector Regression) to see if I could improve performance futher with ML techniques. I found that the version I ran performed worse than the classical time series methods, but I suspect I could improve it by further tuning hyperparameters and including more variables.
